Part 2: Detection EDA Report

Bounding boxes + spatial distribution + quality metrics

1. Bounding box properties

{{ fig_p2_bbox_mean_median_bars | safe }}
{{ fig_p2_bbox_ar_donut | safe }}
{{ fig_p2_bbox_area_hist | safe }}
{{ fig_p2_bbox_wh_scatter | safe }}
Remark
  • Bounding boxes are moderate in size and fairly stable. Mean width is 256.7 px and mean height is 231.9 px, with medians close to means (248.0 px, 222.0 px), indicating no extreme central skew.
  • Aspect ratios are mostly landscape. The dataset has 53.7% landscape, 30.4% portrait, and 15.9% square boxes, so most objects are wider than tall.
  • Area distribution is right-skewed. Most boxes lie in the low-to-mid area range, but there is a clear long tail of large boxes (up to 245,021 px^2), meaning object scale diversity is significant.
  • Size categories separate cleanly in width-height space. The scatter plot shows consistent progression from small to medium to large, so the size_category feature is coherent and useful for analysis.
  • Train and test splits are well aligned. Test and train means are close (mean_area: 60,697 vs 61,877 px^2), suggesting low geometric distribution shift between splits.
  • Practical implication for detection modeling. Use multi-scale training/augmentation and ensure anchor or feature pyramid settings can represent both compact and large objects because the scale spread is broad.

Part 3: Segmentation EDA Report

Pixel / shape / boundary + segmentation quality

1. Pixel Analysis

{{ fig_p3_pixel_donut | safe }}
{{ fig_p3_pixel_mean_std_bars | safe }}
Remark
  • Background overwhelmingly dominates at ~83.63%, meaning birds occupy only a small fraction of each image on average.
  • Foreground (bird body) accounts for ~10.58% and boundary (transition region) for ~5.80% of image pixels.
  • The relatively low standard deviations (Fg 2.47%, Bd 1.77%, Bg 3.17%) indicate this pattern is consistent across all 200 breeds — most CUB images contain a small bird against a large background.
  • The foreground-to-boundary ratio (~1.8:1) suggests boundaries are proportionally thick relative to the bird region, which may challenge segmentation models near object edges.
  • For model training, class-imbalance-aware losses (e.g. focal loss, dice loss) are recommended to prevent the background class from dominating gradient updates.

2. Shape Analysis

{{ fig_p3_shape_metrics_bars | safe }}
Remark
  • Convexity mean of 0.739 shows bird silhouettes are moderately convex. They deviate from a perfect convex hull due to protruding features like beaks, tails, and extended legs.
  • Compactness mean of 0.454 (where 1.0 = perfect circle) confirms that bird shapes are far from circular, reflecting elongated bodies, outstretched wings, or varied postures.
  • Eccentricity mean of 0.833 (close to 1.0) further reinforces that most bird masks are significantly elongated rather than round.
  • Low standard deviations across all three metrics (0.053, 0.069, 0.048) indicate that these shape characteristics are relatively consistent across all 200 breeds.
  • These shape properties suggest that segmentation models should be designed to handle elongated, non-circular objects. Anchor-free or deformable approaches may be more effective than circular/square-biased methods.

3. Mask Analysis (Boundary)

{{ fig_p3_boundary_thickness_bar | safe }}
{{ fig_p3_boundary_smoothness_bar | safe }}
{{ fig_p3_boundary_complexity_bar | safe }}
Remark
  • Average boundary thickness of 5.92 px indicates a moderate transition zone between foreground and background. This is expected for hand-drawn or semi-automatic segmentation masks where pixel-perfect edges are difficult to achieve.
  • Smoothness mean of 0.641 (on a 0-1 scale) shows boundaries are reasonably smooth but not perfectly clean. Some degree of jaggedness or irregularity is present, likely due to fine feather details and complex bird contours.
  • Complexity mean of 1046.88 is notably high, indicating that boundary paths are long and intricate relative to foreground area. This reflects the detailed and irregular outlines typical of bird silhouettes.
  • The thickness distribution shows most masks cluster in the 5+ px range, suggesting generally thick annotation borders that introduce uncertainty near object edges.
  • For segmentation tasks, boundary-aware losses (e.g. boundary loss, active contour loss) and post-processing techniques like CRF refinement could help improve edge quality given these boundary characteristics.

4. Quality Analysis

{{ fig_p3_segq_coverage_barh | safe }}
{{ fig_p3_segq_coverage_cv_barh | safe }}
{{ fig_p3_segq_fg_cv_barh | safe }}
Remark
  • Very low avg_coverage (0.164) confirms that birds typically occupy only ~16.4% of the image area. CUB images are scene-level photos with substantial background context, not tightly cropped object cutouts.
  • High coverage_cv (0.505) reveals significant within-breed variation in object scale — the same species can appear at very different sizes/distances across images, making scale-invariant feature extraction important.
  • High fg_cv (0.616) with only 27/200 breeds below 0.50 shows considerable intra-breed variation in foreground proportion, reflecting diverse poses, occlusions, and framing.
  • No breed achieves high coverage (>0.85) or highly stable coverage (cv < 0.10), confirming that size variability is a dataset-wide challenge, not limited to specific breeds.
  • Avg boundary percentage of 5.80% is moderate, indicating transition pixels are present but do not dominate.
  • Practical implication: multi-scale training, robust crop/resize augmentations, and scale-aware architectures are essential to handle the high variability in object coverage and foreground proportion across this dataset.
